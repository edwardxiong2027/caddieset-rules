\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Recent work on the \emph{CaddieSet} corpus~\cite{jung2025caddieset} showed that joint-pose features extracted from
single-camera golf-swing videos can be combined with ball-flight metrics to \emph{predict} shot outcomes with
interpretable models. We take a complementary step: instead of predicting ball trajectories, we ask which
body-posture features at which swing phases are most strongly associated with long versus short drives, and whether
the population of 1{,}757 recorded swings (from eight golfers) decomposes into a small number of interpretable swing
\emph{styles}. Using effect-size ranking (Cohen's $d$) across top-20\% vs.\ bottom-20\% outcome quantiles, we find a
sparse set of pose features with very large effects ($d>1.0$) that are stable across both camera views. Random-Forest
surrogates trained on pose alone reach $\text{CV}\,R^2=0.50$ for driver distance but only $0.19$--$0.33$ for
directional accuracy, indicating that pose explains \emph{power} much better than \emph{accuracy}. A shallow
decision-tree surrogate ($R^2=0.34$, depth$=3$) produces a seven-leaf coaching-rule tree with distance estimates that
range from 145 to 227 yards depending on three posture decisions. Finally, unsupervised clustering in the Face-On
pose space reveals four stable swing archetypes (silhouette $=0.54$) with distinct performance profiles.
Our contributions extend \cite{jung2025caddieset} by (i) shifting from prediction to prescription, (ii) providing a
phase-$\times$-outcome effect-size map, (iii) separating power and accuracy ceilings in the same corpus, and
(iv) introducing a reproducible pipeline and code release.\footnote{Code and figures:
\url{https://github.com/<USER>/caddieset-rules}.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Golf is a sport in which a small number of biomechanical decisions--- made in roughly one second of body motion ---
determine an outcome that rolls out over hundreds of yards. Building quantitative links from swing kinematics to
ball flight has therefore been a long-standing research goal
\cite{hume2005biomechanics,chu2010kinematic,meister2011rotational,macdougall2021putting}. Progress has historically
been limited by two bottlenecks: (a) access to paired pose/outcome data, and (b) interpretability --- coaches can
only act on insights that are expressible in a handful of human-readable rules \cite{rudin2019stop,doshivelez2017interpretability}.

The recent \emph{CaddieSet} dataset~\cite{jung2025caddieset} addresses (a) by releasing 1{,}757 swings with both
phase-segmented joint features and launch-monitor ball metrics. The dataset paper benchmarks several predictors for
trajectory variables and shows that interpretable models are consistent with expert golf knowledge. What the paper
does \emph{not} address is equally interesting: which pose features, at which phases, matter most; whether the
population decomposes into distinct swing styles; and how much of direction accuracy (versus distance) is in principle
recoverable from pose alone.

In this paper we revisit CaddieSet with a \emph{prescriptive} lens. Our guiding questions are:
\begin{description}
    \item[Q1 --- Prescriptive ranking.] For each swing phase $p\in\{0,\dots,7\}$, each camera view
          (Face-On, Down-the-Line), and each outcome (distance, ball speed, direction accuracy, side-spin), which body-posture features separate long/accurate shots from short/errant ones with the \emph{largest effect sizes}?
    \item[Q2 --- Power vs.\ accuracy decoupling.] How do the predictive ceilings of pose-only models differ across
          outcomes? Is the same information enough for both power and accuracy?
    \item[Q3 --- Archetype discovery.] Do swings cluster into a small number of biomechanical styles, and if so, how
          do these archetypes compare on outcome metrics?
    \item[Q4 --- Rule extraction.] Can we compress the top-ranked signals into a shallow, human-readable decision
          tree that a coach could deploy?
\end{description}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarises the pipeline. Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \emph{phase-$\times$-outcome effect-size map} (Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}) that localises biomechanically
          informative signals in both time (swing phase) and viewpoint.
    \item Quantification of a \emph{power--accuracy gap}: pose-only models reach $R^2\approx 0.5$ for distance but
          $0.19$--$0.33$ for directional accuracy, indicating that pose is necessary but insufficient for predicting
          misses (Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}).
    \item Discovery of \emph{four latent swing archetypes} with distinct performance fingerprints
          (Fig.~\ref{fig:archetypes}).
    \item A \emph{seven-leaf coaching rule tree} (Fig.~\ref{fig:tree}) with a cross-validated $R^2=0.34$ using three
          pose features spanning phases 1, 2, 4 and 5.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Golf biomechanics and performance.}
Classical studies using marker-based motion capture established a canonical view of the swing as a kinetic chain in which a delayed pelvis--thorax separation (the ``X-factor'') \cite{cheetham2001xfactor} is built during the backswing and released through the downswing \cite{nesbit2005sixdof,meister2011rotational}. Quantitative reviews \cite{hume2005biomechanics} conclude that both \emph{distance} and \emph{accuracy} emerge from the same kinetic chain, but through different mechanisms: distance is dominated by clubhead speed (driven by upper--lower body separation), while accuracy depends more on impact-phase club geometry. \citet{chu2010kinematic} report that 4--6 biomechanical variables explain $\sim$56\% of driving performance variance in low-handicap golfers, a figure consistent with our $R^2=0.50$ in Section~\ref{sec:ceiling}. \citet{langdown2012footpressure} links stance width to ground-reaction-force patterns that gate distance output.

\paragraph{Pose estimation for sport.}
General-purpose human-pose models \cite{cao2019openpose,sun2019hrnet,xu2022vitpose} have made it practical to extract
joint-level features from single-camera sports video. The \emph{GolfDB} benchmark~\cite{mcnally2019golfdb} introduced
swing-sequencing as a fine-grained video task, and \cite{liao2022maniflow} extended the phase-segmentation problem.
CaddieSet~\cite{jung2025caddieset} is (to our knowledge) the first public corpus pairing phase-segmented joint
features with launch-monitor ball data.

\paragraph{Interpretable ML in sport.}
Random Forests \cite{breiman2001randomforest} are widely used for sport prediction, but shallow trees and
rule lists are preferred when the downstream consumer is a human coach \cite{rudin2019stop}. Effect-size
comparisons \cite{cohen1988statistical} are a standard tool for translating statistical patterns into
training cues. Our methodology uses each of these tools in its native role: Random Forests to estimate the
\emph{ceiling} of what can be learned from pose, effect sizes to rank which phases and features warrant
coaching attention, and a depth-3 tree to compress the finding into a human-usable rule.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
The closest prior work is the CaddieSet paper itself \cite{jung2025caddieset}, which focuses on predictive
benchmarking of 15 expert-defined summary metrics. We are complementary: we use \emph{all} 68 per-phase pose features
directly and shift the research question from \emph{``how well can we predict ball flight''} to
\emph{``what should a golfer change.''}

\section{Dataset and Features}
\label{sec:data}

We use CaddieSet~\cite{jung2025caddieset} without modification. It contains $N=1{,}757$ swings from 8 golfers, split
across two camera views (Face-On $n=924$, Down-the-Line $n=833$) and 8 clubs, with the driver (W1) dominating
($n=1{,}137$, 65\%). Each swing is labelled with five ball-flight outcomes (Distance, Carry, DirectionAngle, SpinBack,
SpinSide, BallSpeed) and 68 body-posture features indexed by a phase prefix $\{0,\dots,7\}$ corresponding to Address,
Takeaway, Mid-backswing, Top-of-backswing, Early-downswing, Pre-impact, Impact, and Finish. Roughly half of the
features are populated only in one camera view; we therefore run all analyses \emph{per-view} to avoid imputation
bias. Summary statistics by club are reproduced in Table~\ref{tab:clubs}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Effect-size ranking}
For each (view, outcome, feature), we split swings into top-20\% and bottom-20\% outcome groups and compute Cohen's
$d = (\bar{x}_\text{top}-\bar{x}_\text{bot})/s_\text{pooled}$ \cite{cohen1988statistical}. Absolute-valued targets are
used for direction error ($|\text{DirectionAngle}|$) and side spin ($|\text{SpinSide}|$) so that magnitude, not side,
is ranked. We report per-phase maxima in Section~\ref{sec:phase-map} and per-feature sorted tables in the appendix.

\subsection{Predictive ceilings}
To estimate the ceiling of what \emph{pose alone} can explain per outcome, we fit Random-Forest regressors
\cite{breiman2001randomforest} (300 trees, \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf}$=5$) on all well-populated
features in a given view, and report 5-fold cross-validated $R^2$. Missing values are median-imputed after
$[0.1\%,99.9\%]$ winsorisation.

\subsection{Unsupervised archetype discovery}
For the Face-On $\times$ driver (W1) subset ($n=613$), we (i) winsorise each feature at the $[1\%,99\%]$ percentiles
to prevent outlier-dominated clusters, (ii) scale with robust z-scores, (iii) embed with UMAP \cite{mcinnes2018umap}
($\texttt{n\_neighbors}=20, \texttt{min\_dist}=0.1$), and (iv) cluster the raw standardised features
(\emph{not} the UMAP embedding) with $K$-means for $K\in\{3,4,5,6\}$, selecting $K=4$ by silhouette and
interpretability.

\subsection{Rule extraction}
We fit a shallow regression tree ($\texttt{max\_depth}=3$, $\texttt{min\_samples\_leaf}=30$) on the same
Face-On $\times$ W1 subset, predicting Distance. The tree is a human-readable surrogate: its cross-validated $R^2$
quantifies how much predictive power survives the compression.

\subsection{Implementation}
All analyses use Python 3.10, \texttt{scikit-learn}~0.24+ \cite{pedregosa2011scikit}, \texttt{umap-learn}, and
\texttt{matplotlib}. Seeds are fixed (42) throughout. Code is released with this paper.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Phase-$\times$-outcome effect-size map}
\label{sec:phase-map}

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} plots the \emph{maximum} $|d|$ across features per (phase, outcome, view) cell, for driver
swings. Three regularities are visible:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Power signals are concentrated late in the backswing and early in the downswing.} In the Face-On
          view the largest effects for Distance and Ball Speed occur at phases 2 (Mid-backswing, $d=1.19/1.15$) and 4
          (Early-downswing, $d=1.36/1.64$). In the Down-the-Line view the peak shifts to phases 0 (Address, $d=1.30$)
          and 3 (Top of backswing, $d=1.54/1.65$).
    \item \textbf{Accuracy signals are dispersed.} Max $|d|$ for direction error never exceeds $0.81$ in either view,
          and the strongest single cell for side-spin ($d=1.34$ at phase 7 Face-On) is roughly half the magnitude of
          the strongest power cell.
    \item \textbf{The two camera views are complementary, not redundant.} Face-On localises distance signals at
          phases 4--5 (downswing); DTL localises them at phases 0--3 (setup and backswing). A two-camera setup is
          likely to be strictly more informative than either alone.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Power--accuracy ceiling gap}
\label{sec:ceiling}

Figure~\ref{fig:ceiling} shows 5-fold CV $R^2$ for the four outcomes in both views. Pose-only models recover
$\approx$50\% of variance for distance and 55--56\% for ball speed. For accuracy outcomes the ceiling is markedly
lower: $R^2=0.19$--$0.33$ for $|$direction error$|$ and $0.12$--$0.20$ for $|$side spin$|$. This is consistent with
the biomechanical intuition that distance is dominated by large-amplitude body motions (coil, weight shift, lead-arm
extension), whereas accuracy depends on millimetre-scale impact geometry (face angle, swing path at impact) that pose
features only indirectly capture.

\centering

         power from accuracy outcomes.}
\label{fig:ceiling}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Four latent swing archetypes}
\label{sec:archetypes}

$K$-means with $K=4$ on the Face-On feature space produces clusters with silhouette $0.54$ and the following
interpretations (details in Fig.~\ref{fig:archetypes}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A0 --- Early Hip Release} ($n=33$, distance $\approx156$ yd). High mid-backswing hip rotation
          ($+3.6\sigma$) combined with reduced hip shift through impact ($-1.2\sigma$). Corresponds to the classical
          ``spin-out'' fault.
    \item \textbf{A1 --- Balanced Coil} ($n=512$, distance $\approx191$ yd). Population mean; mildly positive
          lead-arm angle and shoulder position at mid-backswing. Contains the bulk of long-hitter swings.
    \item \textbf{A2 --- Aggressive Rotator} ($n=32$, distance $\approx183$ yd). Very high \emph{early-downswing}
          hip rotation ($+4.2\sigma$) but controlled upper body. Lowest direction error of any archetype
          ($|\Delta|=3.4^{\circ}$).
    \item \textbf{A3 --- Compact Swing} ($n=36$, distance $\approx167$ yd). Large negative shoulder-location and
          lead-arm angle ($\leq-2\sigma$) signal a short, closed swing arc. Highest side-spin (1\,276 rpm abs),
          indicating accuracy cost.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{A seven-leaf coaching rule tree}
\label{sec:rules}

Figure~\ref{fig:tree} shows a depth-3 regression tree ($R^2_{CV}=0.34$) using only three pose features: mid-backswing
lead-arm angle (\texttt{2-LEFT-ARM-ANGLE}), hip shift at the takeaway (\texttt{1-HIP-SHIFTED}), and
early-downswing hip shift (\texttt{4-HIP-SHIFTED}). A reader-friendly version of the rule list:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!3,colframe=blue!50!black,title={Prescription ruleset (driver, Face-On)}]
\small
\texttt{IF 2-LEFT-ARM-ANGLE > 160.9} \quad\textit{(i.e.\ keep lead arm within 19$^{\circ}$ of straight at mid-BS)} \\
\quad\texttt{AND 1-HIP-SHIFTED > -0.52} \quad\textit{(hips not stuck behind ball at takeaway)} \\
\quad\texttt{AND 4-HIP-SHIFTED > -0.15} \quad\textit{(hips shift toward target in early downswing)} \\
$\Rightarrow$ \textbf{predicted drive $\approx$201 yd} (vs.\ 145 yd for the worst-case leaf). \\[2pt]

The best leaf (227 yd) additionally requires that the trail-arm angle pre-impact (\texttt{5-RIGHT-ARM-ANGLE}) be
$\leq 153.5^{\circ}$ --- i.e.\ the trail arm is still bent, indicating lag is preserved into the downswing.
\end{tcolorbox}

These rules match textbook coaching language while being both data-driven and reproducible. The distribution plots
in Figure~\ref{fig:distributions} show that for each rule feature, the top-20\% and bottom-20\% distance groups are
cleanly separated in modes, not just means.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why $R^2$ for accuracy is so much lower.}
The $\sim\!2\times$ gap between power and accuracy ceilings (Fig.~\ref{fig:ceiling}) is a substantive finding, not
just a numerical one. Accuracy on a driver is essentially determined by club-face angle and swing path at impact ---
variables that the current CaddieSet features only indirectly capture. Adding clubhead-plane or face-angle signals
(e.g.\ via a secondary high-speed camera at impact) would likely close much of the gap; without them, pose alone
should be used for \emph{distance} coaching and only as a weak accuracy signal.

\paragraph{From effect sizes to coaching language.}
Across both views, three phase-aware themes dominate the biomechanics of long drives: (i) lead-arm extension through
mid-backswing, (ii) hip lateral shift toward the target in the early downswing, and (iii) preserved trail-leg flex
through the top of the backswing. The depth-3 tree in Section~\ref{sec:rules} operationalises themes (i) and (ii)
directly. Theme (iii) appears as the dominant DTL signal in Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap} at phase 3 ($d=-1.40$).

\paragraph{Archetypes as coaching groups.}
The four archetypes in Section~\ref{sec:archetypes} are not just clustering artefacts: A0 and A3 appear to describe
distinct faults (early hip rotation and a collapsed arm arc) while A2 is notable as an \emph{efficient} style --- not
the longest, but consistently on-line. A practical use is matching a new golfer's swing to the nearest archetype and
using archetype-specific rules.

\paragraph{Comparison with the original CaddieSet paper.}
\citet{jung2025caddieset} benchmark 15 expert-defined metrics and establish that interpretable predictors are
consistent with domain knowledge. Our work is complementary and does not reproduce their pipeline: we use all 68
raw per-phase features directly; we rank features by effect size rather than feature importance; and we produce a
decision tree whose leaves are coaching prescriptions rather than trajectory predictions.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Small golfer pool.} $N_\text{golfer}=8$. Archetypes are partly golfer-specific (A3 is 89\% Golfer 4;
          A0 is 82\% Golfer 8). Replication on a larger corpus is essential before drawing population-level claims.
    \item \textbf{Observational data.} All associations are correlational. A straighter lead arm
          \emph{could} be a cause of distance, or merely a marker of a golfer who already has the coil to support it.
          Interventional studies are needed to establish causal coaching value.
    \item \textbf{Feature-name inference.} The public dataset column names include directional descriptors
          (e.g.\ \texttt{HIP-SHIFTED}, \texttt{HANGING-BACK}) but not formal units; sign interpretation follows the
          most natural biomechanical reading.
    \item \textbf{No club-face or path data.} As noted above, the accuracy ceiling is capped by features the
          dataset does not include.
    \item \textbf{Single-view models in practice.} Real deployments will typically record only Face-On or DTL.
          Our cross-view complementarity result implies that either view alone leaves meaningful signal on the table.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility}

The code, figure-generation scripts and this manuscript source are released under an MIT licence at the GitHub

\section{Conclusion}

The CaddieSet corpus \cite{jung2025caddieset} is a rich substrate for biomechanical analysis well beyond trajectory
prediction. By (i) shifting the question from ``predict the ball'' to ``prescribe the swing'', (ii) reporting
per-phase effect sizes, and (iii) compressing the findings into a human-readable rule tree and a four-archetype
clustering, we show that a small number of pose features drive most of the distance gap between long and short
drives --- and that the same features are \emph{not} sufficient for predicting accuracy, motivating future sensor
integration. We hope this secondary analysis encourages further prescriptive, interpretable use of the CaddieSet
data.
